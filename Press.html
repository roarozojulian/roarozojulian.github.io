I write opinion pieces for Econometría Consultores, mostly published in La República, a Colombian economic newspaper. These pieces deal with the economic outlook of Colombia and Latin America. Some, more closely tied to my research, have also appeared in Vox-EU.

Confidence crisis?

August 25, 2023

The Colombian economy is converging to its pre-pandemic trend faster than expected, yet investment (gross fixed capital formation) has fallen roughly 10%, led by declines in "Other buildings and structures" and "Housing." This drop is driven primarily by a confidence crisis and elevated uncertainty — reflected in stock market volatility and Fedesarrollo's negative confidence indices — rather than by restrictive monetary policy, as Brazil's case illustrates.

Is the economy boosted by wages or profits?

June 16, 2023

The labor reform debate in Colombia centers on whether raising wages will increase informality and destroy jobs — as critics and a Banco de la República study suggest (estimating around half a million jobs lost) — or whether higher wages will boost aggregate demand, production, and employment, as Keynesian defenders argue. Using Goodwin's Marxist cycle model and its extension by Bhaduri and Marglin, the key question is whether Colombia's economy is profit-led (where higher wages hurt investment) or wage-led (where higher wages stimulate demand), and both graphical evidence and the majority of academic studies — including the most recent and robust one — indicate Colombia is a profit-led economy, lending support to the critics' predictions.

Where is the potential?

May 5, 2023

The authors propose a new trend-cycle decomposition that allows for hysteresis — shocks affecting both components simultaneously — published as a working paper by Banco de la República. Applying it to 80+ countries, they find COVID-19 caused long-term GDP losses of 1-4% globally, with over half of countries showing GDP above potential, suggesting demand-driven inflation. In Latin America, Colombia recovered strongly, nearly closing its gap, while Mexico remains about five percentage points below its pre-crisis trend.

2023: A difficult year

January 13, 2023

Colombia faces a tough 2023. Growth is revised down from 2% to 1%, as household consumption — the main economic driver in 2021–2022 — stalled by late 2022, with a 2.2% drop reminiscent of the late-1990s crisis. Inflation is expected to close between 7–8%, pressured by a weak peso and tight monetary policy. Unemployment may rise to 11.8%, masking deeper labor market weakness. Fiscal and external risks add uncertainty, while proposed reforms to healthcare, pensions, and energy could worsen the outlook.

A dark storm cloud rises in the sky

October 21, 2022

The IMF's latest outlook warns of a global slowdown in 2023. Colombia closed 2022 strong (~8% growth), but analysts consistently underestimated both growth and inflation. For 2023, inflation will likely remain high (7–8%) due to contractionary monetary policy abroad, rising gasoline prices, and minimum wage hikes—well above the IMF's 5.3% forecast. Growth estimates range from 0.7% to 2.2%, with significant uncertainty. The column urges avoiding easy temptations like capital controls or price freezes, and instead recommends continued rate hikes and inflation-adjusted transfers to protect the most vulnerable.

Why is Colombia growing so much?

August 12, 2022

Colombia's exceptionally high growth rates aren't well explained by traditional macro factors. Public spending during the pandemic was moderate compared to peers, and monetary policy, while accommodative, wasn't uniquely expansive. Two underappreciated drivers stand out: first, the 2021 national strike made fuel price hikes politically impossible, effectively subsidizing transport-dependent industries via the FEPC and boosting output. Second, WOM's entry into telecoms drove down prices precisely when remote work surged, making digital adoption cheaper and catalyzing a technological shift across sectors.

Implications of the Russia–Ukraine war

March 11, 2022

The Russia-Ukraine war fuels global inflation by disrupting oil, gas, and agricultural exports (corn, wheat, urea). Colombia faces food inflation above 15% and total inflation likely over 7% by end of 2022, forcing the central bank to raise rates above 7%. However, not everything is negative: the Colombian peso has appreciated ~150 pesos since the war began, driven by higher oil prices and Latin America emerging as an investment haven. Improved fiscal and current account outlooks offer relief, though structural reforms remain essential.

Circular economy: an approach that generates profitability

February 25, 2022 · With Oscar Rodriguez, Erika Nieves, Guillermo Rudas, Paula Rivera

A study conducted by Econometría for CEPAL, with support from the EU and Euroclima, built a dynamic macroeconomic model to assess the economic effects of reducing the use of plastics, metals, construction materials, and fuels in the Pacific Alliance countries (Chile, Colombia, Mexico, and Peru). Results show that circular economy transitions yield positive and growing effects on GDP and employment. In a conservative scenario—reducing plastic use by 8% and other materials by 5% by 2030—GDP gains range from 0.8% to 2.4%, employment rises 1.1%–1.9%, and GHG emissions fall up to 6.7%.

The 2021 surge in inflation: A look at sticky prices

Vox-EU · November 28, 2021

Sticky-price inflation is inflation in the CPI components with infrequent price changes—where price setters are typically more forward looking. A look at sticky-price inflation provides relevant insights currently: it can help set aside movements in the relative price of goods, and it may also provide information on future CPI inflation. While annual inflation in 2021 is distorted with base effects, these effects can be corrected using monthly or bimonthly inflation, the former being a more opportune measure of inflation than biannual inflation. In October 2021, monthly sticky price inflation, partially corrected for base effects, was 5.3 percent. In turn, the CPI inflation forecast for October 2022 is 5.6 percent and highly uncertain.

Should interest rates really be raised?

September 17, 2021

This column questions whether Colombia's central bank should raise interest rates in September 2021. While Taylor Rule models support a hike—given inflation near the upper target band, peso depreciation, and GDP growth above 7%—I argued that the data alone shouldn't guide the decision. Much of the inflationary pressure stems from a supply shock in food prices caused by the national strike (28A blockades), not from overheating demand. Moreover, employment remains stagnant, unlike GDP's V-shaped recovery, suggesting monetary stimulus is still needed. Raising rates now would unnecessarily punish the economy for a temporary, external shock.

Economic growth and the CONPES recovery framework

March 5, 2021

Colombia's COVID-19 crisis caused a 6.8% GDP drop, record-low inflation, and 16% unemployment. Growth forecasts (~4.5% for 2021) suggest pre-pandemic output won't return until 2022. The CONPES 4023 recovery plan has strengths—especially its focus on infrastructure-driven job creation and extending Ingreso Solidario—but raises concerns: the timeline for generating 2.5 million jobs is unclear, and it lacks active employment policies for women and youth, groups disproportionately hit by the crisis.

We'll remain in 2020 until June

January 22, 2021

In early 2021, economic uncertainty in Colombia was high. Growth projections ranged from 0.8% to 8%, with Econometría estimating 4.5%. Recovery hinged largely on the vaccination plan, whose feasibility was doubtful: Colombia would need 146,000 daily doses, a figure very few countries had achieved. The first half of 2021 was expected to remain stagnant, resembling late 2020, with a meaningful reactivation only in the second half once high-risk populations were vaccinated. The government was urged to provide greater clarity on the vaccination timeline.

Between the Nike curve and the W curve

October 30, 2020

Colombia's economic recovery follows a Nike-curve shape rather than a V—GDP falling ~7-8% in 2020 with only partial rebound in 2021. Labor market and inflation data from August-September suggest the downturn may be slightly less severe than IMF forecasts, driven by the informal sector's quick return once lockdowns eased. However, this reopening raises the risk of a second COVID wave, as seen in France, Spain, and even Sweden. A second wave would trigger a W-shaped curve—economic contraction with or without new lockdowns—making self-care measures essential to protect the recovery.

A V-shaped recovery?

August 7, 2020

Colombia's Ministry of Finance forecasts a V-shaped recovery — a 5.5% GDP drop in 2020 followed by 6.6% growth in 2021. However, most analysts expect a slower, Nike-swoosh recovery, with pre-crisis output levels returning only between 2022 and 2025. An overly optimistic forecast is dangerous: it leads to weaker fiscal stimulus and masks the true scale of debt and deficit ratios relative to GDP. Mixed signals from unemployment and historically low non-tradable inflation further undermine the V-shaped thesis. In a severe crisis, planning for the worst scenario is safer — the social costs of a weak fiscal response far outweigh those of an aggressive one.

Extreme measures for extreme times

April 18, 2020

The coronavirus is both a health and economic crisis. Conventional monetary policy—rate cuts, quantitative easing—proves insufficient because normal transmission channels break down during quarantine and uncertainty. Fiscal responses are essential: payroll subsidies, loan guarantees, and cash transfers help, but mostly reach formal workers. For informal workers, broader transfers are needed and feasible. Ultimately, "helicopter money"—central bank financing of public spending—is a viable option given low inflation expectations and Colombia's oil-revenue shortfall, without undermining central bank credibility.

The coronavirus attacks the lung of the economy

March 20, 2020 · With Mauricio Olivera

The COVID-19 pandemic hit Colombia's economy through simultaneous supply and demand shocks, compounded by the oil price war between Saudi Arabia and Russia. The sharp currency devaluation won't boost exports since oil prices and external demand collapsed. Short-term policy must focus on containment and protecting vulnerable populations through fiscal relief. Monetary policy should consider using international reserves to stabilize the exchange rate. Once the crisis passes, aggressive fiscal and monetary measures—including relaxing the fiscal rule—will be needed to drive recovery. Extreme times demand extreme measures.

2020: More of the same?

January 24, 2020

2019 was a bad year for the global economy — only 11% of world GDP saw faster growth, dragged down by Brexit and the US-China trade war. Latin America suffered too: Argentina in crisis, Mexico and Paraguay stagnant, Chile and Bolivia shaken by political unrest. Colombia stood out with 3.2% growth, but unemployment topped 10% and the current account deficit hit -4% of GDP. For 2020, the outlook is more of the same: ~3.3% growth, persistent unemployment, stable interest rates, and inflation near the upper target band.

Latin America in flames

November 1, 2019 · With Mauricio Olivera

Latin America is in crisis. After the lost decade of the 80s, pro-market reforms of the 90s failed expectations, leading to left-wing governments that rode the commodities boom to expand social programs. When commodity prices fell in 2014, growth collapsed and structural issues—inequality and low productivity—persisted. The pendulum swung back to pro-market governments, but adjustment efforts in Ecuador, Argentina, Chile, and others have also fallen short, sparking widespread unrest. Amid this turmoil, Colombia stands out with solid growth and institutional stability, though inclusive policies remain urgently needed.

Latin America won't grow in 2019

July 26, 2019 · With LAECO

LAECO, the Latin American Alliance of Economic Consultancies, projects that the region's GDP will contract by -0.1% in 2019, downgrading its March estimate by 1.1 percentage points. Recessions in Argentina and Venezuela, plus slowdowns in Brazil, Mexico, Ecuador, and Uruguay, drive this stagnation. These forecasts are more pessimistic than those of the IMF or World Bank. Colombia, while not growing at desired rates, outperforms most peers. Efficient public spending and supportive monetary policy could help weather the regional storm.
